## The standard every score must meet (KV's global defensibility rule)

Every score/rank/rating/percentile/grade/label/verdict must, at the point of display, state: **(1) HOW** it's computed from raw data; **(2) WHY** that construction is sound; **(3) whether it's FAIR** for its objective (like-for-like; peer/category-relative where opportunity sets differ; no mandate/size/survivorship/look-ahead contamination); **(4) if it implies PREDICTION, the data proof** — else labelled *positioning/diagnostic, not a forward signal*. Hand-picked thresholds and labels the math doesn't support are defects.

- **➤ Upgraded explanation (ship this):** CROWD ALIGNMENT (HERDING) & TURNOVER — diagnostic of trading STYLE, NOT a forward signal.

DEFINITION. Herding score (-1 to +1) tells you whether this fund tends to trade in the SAME direction as the rest of the mutual-fund industry, or AGAINST it, in the same month. +1 = it consistently bought what peers were buying and sold what they were selling (a crowd-follower). -1 = it consistently traded opposite the crowd (independent/contrarian). 0 = no consistent alignment. Turnover = how much of the fund's equity book it actively reshuffles per year.

METHOD (reproducible). For each month and each stock the fund holds, we compute the rupees the manager ACTUALLY moved = end value - start value*(1 + the stock's total return). Subtracting that '(1+return)' term removes pure price drift and corporate actions (splits, bonuses, mergers), so a stock merely going up does not look like buying. Worked example: a fund held Rs 100cr of a stock that returned +10%; if it did nothing the holding would be Rs 110cr. If it actually ended at Rs 120cr, the active flow is 120 - 110 = +Rs 10cr bought. We then build the 'crowd' for that stock = the summed active flow of every OTHER fund (the fund itself is excluded, so it cannot agree with itself). We keep only stocks where both the fund's and the crowd's move exceed Rs 0.5cr (a noise floor). For each such stock, agreement = +1 if the fund and the crowd moved the same way, -1 if opposite. The month's herding score is the average of those agreements weighted by the size of the fund's own trades (big bets count more). We average those monthly scores over the trailing ~18 months. Turnover = (half of the total absolute active flow) / end-of-month assets, averaged over months and annualized (one-way %). Data source: all-AMC monthly holdings (holdings_history.parquet) crossed with our NSE total-return panel (tr_returns_monthly.parquet), in funds_flows.build_fund_series.

WHY IT MATTERS. It answers 'is this manager an independent thinker or a crowd-follower, and how actively does she trade?' Academic work (Verardo, Journal of Finance, 'Does Herding Behavior Reveal Skill?') finds anti-herding institutions have historically out-earned herding ones - which is WHY this lens is interesting. But that is a finding from the literature on US institutions, NOT something we have re-validated on our own Indian fund panel.

IS IT FAIR / WHAT IT IS NOT. (1) It is purely CONTEMPORANEOUS - the fund's trade and the crowd's trade are in the SAME month. So we can say a fund trades WITH or AGAINST the crowd, but we CANNOT call it a 'leader' - we have run no test that its moves come EARLIER than the crowd's or predict returns. Read a low-herding fund as 'independent', not 'ahead'. (2) This is a DIAGNOSTIC of process/positioning, NOT a forward-tested buy/sell signal. We do NOT currently show a hard predictive number for it on our data; any such validation is pending and, until it is reproducible from a script here, is not claimed. (This mirrors how peer Active Share is shown as predictive ONLY in the four categories where we actually verified it.) (3) The 'vs peers' percentile compares the fund against ALL funds in one pool; because thinly-traded small-cap/sector funds face a different, sparser 'crowd' than broad large-cap funds, the percentile is best read WITHIN the fund's own SEBI category - a within-category percentile is the correct fix and is planned. (4) Same-AMC funds and closet-index funds can co-trade mechanically, which can inflate apparent 'herding' without genuine following. (5) The metric is corporate-action- and price-drift-immune by construction and uses the full current live fund universe, so survivorship bias does not distort it. Turnover is a STYLE descriptor (how active is the manager), not a quality verdict - high turnover is neither good nor bad on its own.

### `sharpe-sortino-calmar` — Risk-adjusted returns (Sharpe, Sortino, Calmar)
- **Where:** vistas/analytics.py:108-134 (compute) | static/vistas.js:876-879 (render)
- **Fair verdict:** mostly-fair | **Prediction status:** mislabeled
- **How:** Per-series, computed in vistas/analytics.py:108-134 (`_stats_for`) over the active window+frequency; rendered in the COMP table at static/vistas.js:876-879 via `num(s.sharpe)`, `num(s.sortino)`, `num(s.calmar)`.

Raw inputs: `ret` = simple period returns `px.pct_change()` (daily, or weekly W-FRI last); `level` = the rebased TR-index level; `ppy` = 252 daily / 52 weekly (analytics.py:48); `rf_annual` default 0.0 (app.py:98, body-overridable); `rf_per = rf_annual/ppy` (analytics.py:115).

SHARPE (analytics.py:123): `sharpe = (mean - rf_per) * ppy / (std * sqrt(ppy))`, guarded `if std>0 else NaN`. mean,std = `ret.mean()`, `ret.std()` (pandas std = sample, ddof=1). Algebraically this is `(mean - rf_per)*sqrt(ppy)/std` = (annualized mean excess return)/(annualized vol). Annualized-vol = `std*sqrt(ppy)` matches the Vol column (analytics.py:122).

SORTINO (analytics.py:124): same numerator `(mean - rf_per)*ppy`, denominator `downside*sqrt(ppy)` where `downside = sqrt(mean( min(ret - rf_per, 0)^2 ))` (analytics.py:117). Critically the divisor inside `np.mean` is the FULL N (up-periods contribute a squared deviation of 0, but still count in N) — this is the textbook target-downside-deviation / lower-partial-moment convention, NOT a downside-count divisor. Guarded `if downside>0 else NaN`.

CALMAR (analytics.py:125): `cagr / abs(maxdd)` where `cagr = (level_end/level_start)**(365/calendar_days) - 1` (analytics.py:97-104) and `maxdd = (nav/nav.cummax() - 1).min()` over the window (analytics.py:119). Guarded `if maxdd<0 else NaN`.

Rolling Sharpe (RISK tab, analytics.py:185-187) uses the same formula on a trailing window with min_periods = 80% of window, seeded from the extended pre-window frame.

Common-overlap fairness IS enforced at the engine level: analyze() anchors all series to the latest common start date and applies the coverage/continuity gate (CLAUDE.md ★ FAIR COMMON-OVERLAP + MAX_WINDOW_GAP_DAYS=90, analytics.py:54), so the three ratios are computed on the SAME date window for every selected series.
- **Why sound:** The three constructions are textbook-correct and internally consistent:

1) Sharpe — numerator and denominator are annualized on the same basis (mean*ppy over std*sqrt(ppy)), which correctly cancels to mean-excess-return/vol annualized by sqrt(ppy). The sqrt-time vol scaling is the standard iid assumption; rf=0 default is explicit and disclosed. Sound.

2) Sortino — uses the correct full-N target-downside-deviation (Sortino & Price / Bawa-Lindenberg LPM2 with target = rf_per), the convention most institutional risk systems use, NOT the common naive error of dividing only by the count of down-periods (which would inflate the ratio and break comparability with vol). Numerator identical to Sharpe, so Sortino >= Sharpe whenever downside <= total std, which is the intended interpretation. Sound, and arguably done MORE carefully than typical vendor implementations.

3) Calmar — CAGR over absolute max drawdown is the standard Calmar/MAR definition. MaxDD is window-relative (peak resets at window start), consistent with how the Drawdown panel is drawn. Sound as defined.

Guards are correct: each ratio returns NaN (rendered "—") when its denominator is non-positive (std=0, downside=0, or maxdd>=0), so no divide-by-zero blowups. The `_no_inf`/`_clean` helpers null out inf/NaN so a zero prior price can't poison the stats.

The common-overlap window + coverage gate mean these ratios are like-for-like across the selected basket — they are not each computed on a different sample, which is the usual silent unfairness in multi-series scorecards. That is the single most important fairness property and it is correctly enforced upstream.

Where it is NOT a forward signal (the one real overclaim): all three are DESCRIPTIVE summaries of the realized window. They have well-documented sample-dependence — Sharpe is noisy at short windows and rises mechanically as window length grows under positive drift; Calmar/MaxDD are dominated by the single worst path point and are highly window-length- and start-date-sensitive (a window that excludes 2008/2020 shows a flattering Calmar). None of this is wrong for a diagnostic, but it means the number describes the past, it does not forecast the future.
- **Fairness:** Like-for-like: YES at the sample level. - **➤ Upgraded explanation (ship this):** Definition. Three "return-per-unit-of-risk" report-card numbers for the chosen window. Sharpe = how much return you earned for each unit of total bumpiness (ups AND downs). Sortino = the same idea but only counting the bumpiness from LOSSES, so a fund that is volatile only on the way up is not penalised. Calmar = the annual growth rate divided by the worst peak-to-trough fall you would have lived through in the window.

Method (each over the active window and frequency; one row per series, all rows computed on the SAME common date window so they are comparable — vistas/analytics.py:108-134).
- ppy = periods per year = 252 (daily) or 52 (weekly). rf = the risk-free rate, DEFAULT 0% (so by default these are return-vs-risk ratios, not return-vs-risk-PREMIUM ratios; you can set rf in the request to get the academic version).
- Sharpe = (average period return − rf_per) × ppy ÷ (stdev of returns × √ppy). In plain terms: annualised average excess return ÷ annualised volatility. Worked: a daily series with mean +0.05%/day and 1% daily stdev → annual return ≈ 0.05%×252 = 12.6%, annual vol ≈ 1%×√252 = 15.9%, Sharpe ≈ 0.79.
- Sortino = same numerator, but the denominator is "downside deviation" = √(average of squared NEGATIVE returns, where up-days count as zero but still count in the average). This is the proper target-downside-deviation convention (full sample size in the divisor, not just the down-days), so it is directly comparable to volatility. Sortino is always ≥ Sharpe.
- Calmar = CAGR ÷ |MaxDD|. CAGR = (end level/start level)^(365/calendar days) − 1; MaxDD = the most negative value of (level ÷ its own running peak − 1) inside the window. Worked: 14% CAGR with a −28% worst drawdown → Calmar = 0.5.
- Each shows "—" when its denominator is zero/non-negative (no volatility, no down-days, or no drawdown).

Why. CAGR alone hides the ride. Two series can compound the same but one halves on the way; these three numbers say "how much reward did you get for the pain you had to sit through" — total-risk pain (Sharpe), loss-only pain (Sortino), and worst-case pain (Calmar).

Is it fair? Mostly — with two honest caveats. (1) These are DIAGNOSTIC, REALIZED summaries of the past window, NOT a forward signal: a high Sharpe/Sortino/Calmar does not, on our data, predict a higher future ratio (we run no lead-lag/out-of-sample test here), so read them as "what this window felt like," not "what to buy." (2) They are window-dependent: Sharpe is noisy and tends to look better over longer/calmer windows, and Calmar swings hard depending on whether your window includes a crash (2008/2020) — so only compare ratios computed over the same, long-enough, representative window (which this terminal does enforce: every row uses the common overlap window with a continuity gate, so series are like-for-like and there is no look-ahead). The default rf=0 makes all ratios a touch flattering versus the textbook ~6-7% risk-free version; set rf if you want the strict definition.

DEFINITION (plain English): How much risk-adjusted "active" return a manager's stock picks earned ABOVE the right benchmark, per unit of how much they wandered from it. Each month we compute the fund's "active return" A = (fund's holdings return) − (its SEBI-category index return). The Information Ratio is the average of A divided by how bumpy A is, scaled to a yearly number. High IR = the manager consistently beat the benchmark by a steady margin; low/negative = they didn't, or did it erratically.

WORKED EXAMPLE: if a fund beats its benchmark by an average of +0.4% per month and the month-to-month wobble of that gap is 0.9%, then monthly IR = 0.4/0.9 = 0.44, and annualised IR = 0.44 × √12 ≈ 0.51. An IR of ~0.5 means it would take about 16 years of data before we could statistically PROVE the skill wasn't luck (t-stat = IR × √years; we need t ≥ 2; (1.96/0.5)² ≈ 15 years). That is why a "Years to prove skill" number sits next to it.

METHOD (reproduce it): Data = the all-AMC monthly holdings store ⋈ each holding's next-month TOTAL return ⋈ the fund's SEBI-category total-return index. One row = one fund-month. Fund return rp = Σ (start-of-month weight × that stock's next-month total return), over the equity sleeve renormalised to 100% (we require ≥80% of weight to have a return that month). Benchmark return rb = next-month total return of the category index (Large Cap→NIFTY 100, Mid→MIDCAP 100, Small→SMALLCAP 250, diversified→NIFTY 500). Active return A = rp − rb. IR = mean(A) × 12 ÷ (stdev(A) × √12). Filters: funds with under 24 months show NO IR (too short). The "skilled" verdict additionally needs t ≥ 2 AND a bootstrap (which DOES correct for month-to-month correlation) showing the average active return is positive in ≥95% of resamples.

IMPORTANT — what this IR is and isn't: (1) It is GROSS and based on the PAPER portfolio (the holdings), so it is BEFORE fees, cash drag and trading costs — it measures the manager's picks, not the post-fee return you actually earned. (2) The plain t-stat and "years to prove" shown here assume each month is independent; the stricter "skilled" verdict corrects for that with a bootstrap, so trust the VERDICT over the raw t. (3) For sectoral/thematic funds the benchmark is the broad market, so their IR mixes a sector bet with selection — read it as positioning, not pure skill.

IS IT FAIR? Mostly. It is like-for-like on the benchmark: each fund is judged against ITS OWN category index, which removes the cap-size advantage/penalty so a small-cap manager isn't flattered by the small-cap wave. No look-ahead (weights precede returns). Caveats: it is gross (not net of fees), it does not yet show where the fund's IR ranks WITHIN its category (so a lone number lacks peer context), and it is not survivorship-adjusted. 

PREDICTION? This is a DIAGNOSTIC of the PAST, not a forward signal. A high IR with t ≥ 2 is good evidence the past edge wasn't luck — it does NOT mean we have tested, on our data, that high-IR funds keep winning (we have not run an IR-persistence test, unlike the active-share panel which states whether higher active share has historically led to higher subsequent peer-relative return). Read IR as "did this manager add risk-adjusted value so far, and is that real or noise" — not "this fund will outperform next."

WHY IT MATTERS: NAV is the outcome; the holdings are the cause. IR reads the manager's selection-and-sizing edge straight off their decisions and tells you whether it's big and steady enough to be more than luck — separating a genuine process from a lucky run, while being explicit that it's a verdict on the past, gross of costs.

The companion `cyclical_caveat` (lines 526-528) surfaces a cycle flag (peak/elevated-margin/above-trend) next to it so a low multiple on peak earnings is tagged a possible value trap. The whole panel sits under a header already labelled 'Research snapshot - rule-based, diagnostics only' (vistas.js:1433).
- **Why sound:** Partly sound, with one real construction flaw. SOUND parts: PEG itself (multiple / growth-rate) is a textbook, well-understood valuation-to-growth ratio; the divide-by-zero / negative-growth guard is correct (a PEG on negative earnings or negative growth is meaningless, and the code blanks it rather than printing garbage); pairing the ratio with the Quality score, the 10y P/E percentile and an explicit cyclical-trap caveat is exactly the right framing (the code's own `why_useful` notes a low multiple on a declining/peak-cyclical business is a trap, not a bargain). NOT FULLY SOUND: (a) TIME-AXIS MISMATCH - the numerator uses TTM earnings (most recent 12 months) but the denominator's growth endpoints are annual fiscal-year PAT (latest FY vs FY-5), so the multiple and the growth are measured on different earnings bases; for a fast-changing company these don't line up. (b) ENDPOINT-ONLY CAGR - `_cagr` uses only the first and last annual PAT, so a single abnormal base or terminal year (a one-off gain, an impairment, a COVID trough) swings g5 hard and therefore swings PEG hard; there is no smoothing, no fit, no check that the path between endpoints was monotone. (c) BACKWARD-LOOKING GROWTH IN A FORWARD RATIO - the canonical PEG that practitioners trade on uses FORWARD (expected) EPS growth; this implementation substitutes TRAILING 5y PAT CAGR, which is a different (and weaker) quantity - high past growth is the classic mean-reverter. The prose ('cheap if growth holds', stock_intel.py:533) implicitly assumes past growth persists, which is the exact assumption a backward CAGR cannot support. (d) PAT not EPS - growth is on net profit, not per-share, so it ignores dilution/buybacks; a company that grew PAT 15%/yr while issuing 10%/yr of stock has far lower per-share growth, yet PEG looks the same.
- **Fairness:** NOT like-for-like across the cross-section for a comparative objective, though it is internally consistent for a single name. (1) NO PEER/CATEGORY NORMALIZATION: PEG is an absolute number with hand-picked cutoffs (>2 expensive, <1 cheap, stock_intel.py:533). Fair PEG levels differ massively by sector and rate regime - a 1.5 PEG is cheap for a structural compounder (consumer/IT) and expensive for a commodity cyclical; the terminal applies one fixed scale to all, so the same PEG flatters a low-growth defensive and penalizes a hyper-grower. The thresholds are NOT data-derived. (2) SURVIVORSHIP / BASE-YEAR contamination via the endpoint CAGR: if FY-5 was a depressed/loss-near-zero base, g5 explodes and PEG collapses to look artificially cheap; if FY-5 was a one-off peak, g5 is tiny and PEG looks artificially expensive. (3) NO LOOK-AHEAD: good - it uses only trailing data, so no future leakage. (4) SECTOR-FIT: the cyclical caveat partially mitigates the worst trap (peak-margin cheapness) but does NOT rescale the >2/<1 verdict by sector. (5) BANK handling: the surrounding panel correctly drops EV/EBITDA for banks (vistas.js:1484), but PEG itself is shown for banks where P/E and PAT growth are distorted by provisioning cycles - no bank-specific caveat on the PEG line. Net: fair as a within-stock 'what growth am I paying for' diagnostic; UNFAIR if read as a cross-stock cheap/expensive ranking on its fixed scale.
- **Defects:**
  - Time-axis mismatch: numerator is TTM P/E (most recent 12 months) but denominator is annual fiscal-year PAT CAGR (FY vs FY-5) - the multiple and the growth are measured on different earnings bases (stock_intel.py:524 dividing val.pe_now by fundamentals _cagr(pat,5)).
  - Endpoint-only 5y CAGR (_cagr lines 171-178 uses only pat[-1] and pat[-6]): a single abnormal base or terminal year (one-off gain, impairment, COVID trough) swings g5 and therefore PEG hard; no smoothing, no path check, no monotonicity test.
  - Backward-looking growth in a forward ratio: the standard tradable PEG uses FORWARD expected EPS growth; this substitutes TRAILING 5y PAT CAGR (a known mean-reverter). The prose 'cheap if growth holds' (stock_intel.py:533) silently assumes past growth persists, which trailing CAGR cannot establish.
  - Growth on PAT not EPS: ignores dilution/buybacks; per-share economics can differ materially from net-profit growth, so PEG can mislead for serial issuers/repurchasers.
  - Hand-picked, non-sector-relative thresholds (>2 expensive / <1 cheap, stock_intel.py:533) applied uniformly to all stocks; fair PEG levels differ by sector and rate regime, and the cutoffs are not data-derived. No peer/category normalization anywhere.
  - Base-year fragility = a fairness/contamination risk: a depressed FY-5 base inflates g5 and makes PEG look artificially cheap (the classic value trap the panel warns about elsewhere but does not quantitatively guard in the PEG number itself).
  - Bank case: PEG is shown for banks (no bank-specific caveat on the PEG line) even though bank P/E and PAT growth are distorted by provisioning cycles, while the same panel correctly drops EV/EBITDA for banks (vistas.js:1484).
- **➤ Upgraded explanation (ship this):** Definition - PEG ('P/E to Growth') asks: for every 1% of profit growth this company has delivered, how many turns of earnings is the market making me pay? It is the price-to-earnings multiple divided by the profit growth rate. A PEG near 1 roughly means 'the price already bakes in the growth'; below 1 looks cheap IF that growth continues; above 2 means a lot of future growth is already paid for. Method - We compute PEG = (current P/E) / (5-year profit growth %). The P/E is Screener's trailing-twelve-month multiple = today's price divided by the last 12 months of earnings per share (fundamentals.py:636). The growth figure is the 5-year compound annual growth rate of ANNUAL net profit, taken end-to-end: (latest full-year profit / the profit five years earlier) ^ (1/5) - 1, expressed as a percent (fundamentals.py:360, _cagr lines 171-178). Worked example: a stock at 30x earnings that grew net profit ~15%/yr over 5 years has PEG = 30 / 15 = 2.0. We deliberately leave PEG BLANK when growth is zero or negative, or when earnings are negative, because the ratio is meaningless there (stock_intel.py:524). Why - It reframes a raw P/E: a 30x multiple is dear for a 5%-grower (PEG 6) but reasonable for a 30%-grower (PEG 1). We always show it next to the Quality score, the P/E percentile vs the stock's own 10-year range, and a cyclical-trap warning, because a cheap-looking multiple on a declining or peak-cycle business is a trap, not a bargain. Is it fair / what to trust - This is a DIAGNOSTIC of valuation-vs-history, NOT a forward signal: nothing here has been back-tested to show low-PEG stocks beat high-PEG stocks on our data, and the code makes no such claim. Read it WITHIN a single stock, not as a cross-stock cheap/expensive ranking, because (1) the >2/<1 cutoffs are fixed and not sector-adjusted - a 1.5 PEG is genuinely cheap for a steady compounder but expensive for a commodity cyclical; (2) the growth is BACKWARD-looking (past 5 years), not the forward growth a true PEG wants, and fast past growth tends to fade; (3) it uses only the start and end years, so a one-off depressed or peak base year five years ago can make PEG look artificially cheap or dear - cross-check the multi-year profit path on the Fundamentals tab; (4) the numerator is trailing-12-month earnings while the denominator is full-year-to-full-year growth, so they are on slightly different earnings clocks; and (5) for banks, treat PEG with extra caution because provisioning cycles distort both P/E and profit growth. Bottom line: use PEG to understand HOW MUCH growth the price assumes, then confirm with quality, the cyclical caveat, and the underlying profit trend - it is positioning context, not a buy/sell signal.

## Herding metric — deep data-validation (independently re-verified by me)

**Verdict:** DEMOTE + RELABEL + RECOMPUTE THRESHOLDS. Do NOT keep the current presentation; do NOT drop the metric.

The herding score is a fair, well-constructed, PERSISTENT positioning/style trait (V1: split-half +0.32, YoY +0.31; the corp-action- and drift-immune flow construction is sound). But it FAILS every claim the UI makes about it:
1. NOT a forward signal (V2: forward IC ~0, t between -0.14 and -0.62, decile spread ~0 and slightly negative; same for category-relative V5). The "VALIDATED ... lower herding ~ higher excess return (Spearman -0.10), consistent with Verardo (JF)" text is a contemporaneous, whole-history fund-average artifact (reproduces as +0.12 to +0.15 — opposite sign to the stated -0.10, and even contemporaneously the monthly cross-sectional IC is a coin-flip t=0.5). It was never forward-tested in committed code.
2. The "lead or follow?" / "leader" framing is REFUTED (V3: contrarians have the LOWEST lead score 0.062 and higher contemp than lead — they fade, they don't lead).
3. Thresholds are mis-set (V6: 0.4% of funds qualify as contrarian).
4. The crowd definition is a judgement call that flips 21.6% of labels (V4).

So: it measures what it claims (with/against the crowd) and is persistent and fair AS A DIAGNOSTIC, but every PREDICTIVE/leadership implication is unsupported. Per the defensibility rule, that makes the current labels defects. Keep the number, strip the prediction and leadership claims, relabel as positioning, and fix the bands.

### persistence
V1 VERDICT: herding IS a persistent trait at the multi-month/window level, but NOT month-to-month.

Method (reproducible): I rebuilt the per-fund herding time series over the FULL history (2013-04 to 2026-05, 158 months) using the exact production convention from vistas/funds_flows.py::build_fund_series (lines 327-334): each fund-month, herding = trade-size-weighted mean of sign(fund flow)*sign(ex-self crowd rupee-sum) over stocks the fund actively traded (|flow|>0.5cr, net of price drift via flow = MV_end - MV_start*(1+r_stock)). I verified this rebuild matches the live build_fund_series over the last 18 months to rounding (765 common funds, max abs diff 0.001, mean 0.0001). Panel = 63,900 fund-months, 769 funds.

- SPLIT-HALF (per fund, mean herding in its first half of months vs second half; funds with >=8 months, n=725 funds): Spearman rank-corr = +0.322 (p=5e-19), Pearson +0.25. A fund contrarian-vs-follower early tends to stay so late.
- YEAR-OVER-YEAR (per fund-year mean herding, >=4 months/yr; corr of year y vs y+1, pooled, n=5,301 fund-year pairs): Spearman +0.305 (p=1e-114).
- AR(1) of the RAW MONTHLY series (per-fund demeaned, pooled, funds >=12 months, n=696): only +0.045. Month-to-month herding is essentially noise.

Reading: a single month's herding is unreliable; the 18-month herding_avg the deck shows is exactly what makes it a usable trait (averaging kills the AR(1) noise and surfaces the persistent +0.32 component). Persistence is real and significant.

### forward_predictive
V2 VERDICT: FAILS. Herding does NOT predict forward excess return at any horizon. The "validated edge" claim in the code/UI is false as a FORWARD signal.

Method (reproducible): forward EXCESS return from vistas/funds_attribution.py::load_panel — per (navindia_code, ym) it gives rp (paper, equity-sleeve, covered-weight renormalised) and rb (the SEBI-category benchmark TR), with the 1-month return stamped FORWARD (t->t+1) onto month t. Forward h-month excess at month t = prod(1+rp over t..t+h-1) - prod(1+rb over t..t+h-1), requiring all h months contiguous. Each month I take the cross-sectional Spearman IC between herding(t) and that forward excess (months with >=20 funds). herding(t) uses the trade landing on the t snapshot; rp/A at t is the t->t+1 return -> strictly forward, no look-ahead. t-stat = mean_IC/std*sqrt(n_months).

- h=3m: mean IC -0.0008, t=-0.14, %months>0 = 53.5%, n=155 months
- h=6m: mean IC -0.0021, t=-0.35, %months>0 = 46.1%, n=152
- h=12m: mean IC -0.0040, t=-0.62, %months>0 = 47.9%, n=146
- DECILE SPREAD (contrarian decile, lowest herding, minus herder decile forward excess): -0.02% (3m), -0.03% (6m), -0.13% (12m) -> ~-0.13%/yr annualized at 12m and NEGATIVE, i.e. contrarians earned slightly LESS, not more. %months the spread is positive ~49-53% (coin-flip).
- ERA STABILITY: IC first-half vs second-half is -0.007 vs +0.005 (3m) / -0.008 vs +0.000 (12m) — sign flips across eras, no stable forward signal.

CRITICAL: I traced the live "Spearman -0.10, ~2-3%/yr contrarian-minus-herding, consistent with Verardo" claim (funds_flows.py:296-297, mirrored in static/vistas.js:2698). It is NOT backed by any committed forward test — grep found no script that ever computed a forward IC for herding. It reproduces only as a CONTEMPORANEOUS, whole-history fund-AVERAGE correlation: herding_avg vs same-window mean excess A = Spearman +0.124 (p<0.001, n=723), 18m-window +0.079, and ~+0.15 in BOTH the pre-2020 and 2020+ eras. Note the sign is +0.12 to +0.15 on the current data (higher herding -> higher contemporaneous excess), the OPPOSITE of the -0.10 the UI states — so the UI's stated direction is not even reproducible. It also holds for paper return (+0.157), so it is contemporaneous co-movement (funds whose trades coincided with crowd moves that same window co-moved with returns), not a forward edge. Forward, it is flat zero.

### lead_lag
V3 VERDICT: the "leader" label ("does this manager lead or follow?", vistas.js:2665) is REFUTED. Contrarians do NOT lead the crowd; they fade it contemporaneously and predict the next-month crowd WORSE than followers do.

Method (best-effort, reproducible): from the per-(fund,stock,month) trade-sign table I built (1.09M rows), for each stock-month I set crowd_sign(t) = sign(median ex-self crowd rupee-sum across the funds trading it). Per fund: lead = mean(fund_trade_sign_t * crowd_sign_{t+1}, same stock) — does the fund's trade today predict the rest of the crowd next month; contemp = mean(fund_trade_sign_t * crowd_sign_t) — does it just move with the crowd now. Funds with >=30 such observations (n=754); bucketed by tercile of herding_avg.

- Contrarian tercile (lowest herding): lead = +0.062, contemp = +0.149.
- Follower tercile (highest herding): lead = +0.120, contemp = +0.316.
- Spearman(herding_avg, lead) = +0.36 and Spearman(herding_avg, contemp) = +0.68 — BOTH positive: more-herding funds align with the crowd both now and next month MORE, not less.

If contrarians "led," they would have a HIGH lead score (their trade today anticipates tomorrow's crowd) while a LOW/negative contemp. Instead their lead (0.062) is the LOWEST of all buckets and below their own contemp (0.149) — they are the ones LEAST able to anticipate the crowd. The label has the relationship backwards. A contrarian here is a contemporaneous against-the-crowd trader, which is positioning, not leadership.

### crowd_robustness
V4 VERDICT: moderately robust in ranking, but the two crowd definitions are NOT interchangeable — they disagree on the sign of ~1 in 5 fund-months, so the binary "with/against crowd" label is fragile at the margin.

Method (reproducible): I recomputed herding a second way using the canonical LSV (Lakonishok-Shleifer-Vishny) buyer-FRACTION crowd: for each stock-month, crowd_frac = (#funds buying - #funds selling)/(#buying + #selling) among the OTHER funds (ex-self, headcount, |flow|>0.5cr) — a one-fund-one-vote crowd. herding_frac = trade-size-weighted mean of sign(fund flow)*sign(crowd_frac). Compared to the production signed-RUPEE-sum crowd (one-rupee-one-vote). Per-fund average over full history, common funds (n=769).

- Rank-corr of the two per-fund herding rankings: Spearman +0.672 (p=3e-102), Pearson +0.749.
- Fund-month-level SIGN AGREEMENT (do the two herding signs agree that month?): 78.4% (n=63,769 paired fund-months) — i.e. 21.6% of fund-months flip with/against depending on crowd definition.

Reading: the rupee crowd is dominated by a few large funds; the headcount crowd is one-vote-each. They agree on the broad ordering (0.67) but a fund near the balanced middle can be labelled "with" under one and "against" under the other in a fifth of months. For a 3-band style label this is a real fragility. If kept, the metric should at minimum report both, or use the headcount (LSV) crowd which is the literature-standard and less large-fund-dominated.

### category_relative
V5 VERDICT: demeaning within SEBI category preserves the persistence but does NOT rescue forward prediction — it is the same flat zero. Category-relative herding is no more a forward signal than pooled.

Method (reproducible): herding_cat-rel(fund,month) = herding(fund,month) - mean(herding over that fund's SEBI category that month), then re-ran V1 and V2 on the demeaned series.

- PERSISTENCE (V1 redo): split-half Spearman +0.283 (p=8e-15, n=725); YoY Spearman +0.279 (p=5e-95, n=5,301). Slightly lower than pooled (0.32/0.31) but still clearly persistent — the trait is partly within-category-relative and partly a category-level tilt.
- FORWARD IC (V2 redo): h=3m IC -0.0026 (t=-0.46); h=6m -0.0049 (t=-0.87); h=12m -0.0045 (t=-0.75). %months>0 ~47-51%. Decile spread (contrarian minus herder) ~0 / slightly negative (-0.25%/yr at 12m). Era halves: -0.007 then -0.002 to +0.002 — no stable signal.

Reading: category-relative framing is the RIGHT fairness fix for COMPARING funds (a small-cap book and a large-cap book face different crowds), and it is what should be used for any peer percentile. But it does not create predictive power that isn't there. Demeaning makes the diagnostic fairer, not predictive.

### threshold

DATA-DERIVED PROPOSAL (terciles of herding_avg): "more contrarian than peers" if herding_avg < +0.168 (bottom third); "balanced" if +0.168 to +0.252 (middle third); "consensus follower" if > +0.252 (top third). Because the metric is not forward-predictive, even these bands should be presented as RELATIVE POSITIONING vs peers, not as quality tiers — and ideally computed category-relative (V5) so a fund is ranked against its own category's crowd, not the whole industry.
