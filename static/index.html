GPIndexed performance (NAV, rebased to 100)

Definition · Method · Why

What: each index's total-return level rebased to 100 at the window start, so paths are compared like-for-like from the same footing.

Method: source = the NSE total-return index level (dividends reinvested). Series = level / level[start] × 100. Log toggle plots the y-axis on a log scale so equal % moves look equal. Drag the range slider or box-zoom to focus any sub-window; hover shows all series at a date.

Why: the first read on any desk — who compounded more, and how the lead built up over time.

COMPComparative statistics

Definition · Method · Why

What: the standard return/risk scorecard over the chosen window, one row per series, plus a CAGR / alpha bar.

Method (each over the active window & frequency): Total = level[end]/level[start]−1; CAGR = (level[end]/level[start])365/days−1; Vol = stdev(returns)·√ppy; Sharpe = (mean−rf)·ppy ÷ vol; Sortino uses downside deviation only; MaxDD = min(level/cummax−1); Calmar = CAGR ÷ |MaxDD|; Best/Worst 1Y = max/min trailing-1Y return; α = CAGR(series) − CAGR(primary benchmark). ppy = 252 daily / 52 weekly; CAGR uses 365 calendar days.

Why: CAGR alone is a trap — read return, risk and drawdown together.

αRolling alpha & beta vs benchmark

Definition · Method · Why

Rolling α (Excess): trailing-window return of the index minus the benchmark, annualized when the window ≥ 1Y (else cumulative). Rolling α (Jensen): intercept of r_index − rf = α + β·(r_bench − rf) fit over the trailing window, annualized — the part of return NOT explained by market exposure.

Rolling β: cov(r_index, r_bench) / var(r_bench) over the trailing window — sensitivity to the benchmark.

Why: a single full-period alpha hides when the edge actually showed up; the rolling view exposes regime dependence.

RISKRolling risk

Definition · Method · Why

Drawdown = level/running-max − 1 (the underwater curve). Volatility = rolling stdev(returns)·√ppy. Sharpe = rolling (mean−rf)·ppy ÷ vol. Correlation = rolling corr(index, benchmark). Relative strength = index/benchmark price ratio, rebased to 100.

Why: investor experience lives here — how deep and how long the pain, how bumpy the ride, how the pair co-moves.

CORRCorrelation matrix

Definition · Method · Why

What: the full pairwise correlation of all selected series (indices and benchmarks) with each other — not just vs the benchmark — over the chosen window and frequency.

Method: Pearson correlation of the period (daily) returns for each pair, computed on their common dates. Colour grades continuously: green = high (+1), yellow = mid (0), red = low (−1); the deeper the green, the higher the correlation.

Why: shows which holdings actually diversify each other vs. move together — the rolling line above only compares each index to the benchmark.

CAPUp / down capture

Definition · Method · Why

What: how much of the benchmark's up-moves and down-moves each index captures. Up = Σ(index ret | bench>0) ÷ Σ(bench ret | bench>0); Down = Σ(index ret | bench<0) ÷ Σ(bench ret | bench<0); Ratio = Up ÷ Down.

Why: a great profile captures the upside (Up→high) while ducking the downside (Down→low, Ratio≫1). Points below the 45° line in the scatter are favourable.

CYCalendar-year returns & alpha

Definition · Method · Why

What: total return for each calendar year = year-end level ÷ prior year-end level − 1 (first year uses the window start as base, marked *). Alpha = year return − primary-benchmark year return.

Method: the % positive / % negative under each chart count the calendar years where that series was up vs down (alpha > 0 vs < 0 for the alpha chart).

Why: shows consistency and which regimes helped or hurt, free of any rolling-window smoothing.

SEASMonthly-return heatmap

Definition · Method · Why

What: month-by-year matrix of monthly total returns (month-end ÷ prior month-end − 1) for the selected index. Green = positive, red = negative.

Why: surfaces seasonality and the months that drove or dragged a year.

DISTReturn & alpha distributions

Definition · Method · Why

What: the distribution of rolling, overlapping returns at four horizons — Monthly, Quarterly, Yearly, and 3-Year (the 3Y is a CAGR; the others are cumulative) — drawn as smooth density curves (Gaussian kernel density) rather than bars, so several indices overlay cleanly. Below them, the same four horizons for alpha vs the primary benchmark.

Method: at each date, the trailing-horizon return (e.g. trailing-1-year) is computed; the density curve is the smoothed frequency distribution of those overlapping observations. A horizon longer than the selected window is omitted (only that chart). The y-axis is probability density (area integrates to 1).

Why: shows the full shape of outcomes at each horizon — central tendency, spread, skew and tails — not just a single average.

Valuation metric

VALValuation over time

Definition · Method · Why

What: the actual valuation level (P/E, P/B or dividend yield) of each selected index over the window — not rebased; the number itself is the information.

Method: source = NSE's published daily index P/E, P/B and dividend-yield series. P/E = index price ÷ trailing earnings; P/B = price ÷ book value; Div Yield = trailing dividend ÷ price (%). The dashed band per series is its window mean ±1σ (lighter) and ±2σ — the mean-reversion envelope.

Why: the first valuation read — is this index trading rich or cheap versus where it usually sits, and is it stretching away from its band?

RICH / CHEAPWhere each index sits in its own history

Definition · Method · Why

What: the cheap/rich gauge — for each index, where today's valuation sits within its own history over the window.

Method: percentile = fraction of the window's observations at or below the current value (so 80% means dearer than 80% of its own history); z-score = (current − window mean) ÷ window σ. For P/E and P/B a high percentile = expensive (rich); for dividend yield it inverts — a high yield = cheap. The pin marks the current percentile on the green→red scale.

Why: "is 22× expensive?" only means something relative to that index's own range — this answers it at a glance, and is comparable across indices.

CROSS-SECTIONSnapshot — who is cheapest now

Definition · Method · Why

What: the current valuation of every selected series side by side, ranked, with each one's own-history percentile.

Method: bars = the latest value of each series (most expensive first; for dividend yield, highest yield = cheapest first). The label shows the own-history percentile and the rich/cheap tag.

Why: the relative-value read across your watchlist in one frame — which index is the dear one and which is the bargain right now.

SPREADValuation spread vs primary benchmark

Definition · Method · Why

What: each index's valuation minus the primary benchmark's, over time — the relative-richness series (e.g. Midcap P/E − NIFTY 500 P/E).

Method: spread = index value − primary-benchmark value on each common date. The statline reports the latest spread, its window mean, and its percentile within the window.

Why: absolute valuation drifts with the whole market; the spread isolates whether a segment is getting expensive relative to the broad market — a classic mean-reversion signal.

DISTValuation distribution

Definition · Method · Why

What: the distribution (smooth density) of each index's valuation over the window — the full shape of where it has traded, with the current value marked.

Method: Gaussian kernel density of the window's daily values. A right-shifted current value sits in the expensive tail (for a yield, the cheap tail).

Why: shows whether today is a normal level or an outlier, and how wide the index's valuation range has been.
